Consensus Facts

  • Republican Senators Lindsey Graham (R-SC), Katie Britt (R-AL), and Eric Schmitt (R-MO) announced legislation to authorize $400 million in federal funding to build President Trump's White House ballroom.
  • The legislative push follows a shooting incident at the White House Correspondents' Dinner on Saturday, April 25, 2026, in which a gunman breached a security checkpoint at the Washington Hilton Hotel.
  • Trump was evacuated from the dinner during the incident.
  • Graham framed the ballroom as a national security necessity rather than a vanity project, arguing the president should not have to attend events at less secure venues like the Washington Hilton.
  • Sen. Rand Paul (R-KY) announced separate legislation related to the ballroom but indicated he does not favor using full taxpayer funding, noting Trump has already raised private money.
  • Rep. Lauren Boebert (R-CO) also signaled she would introduce ballroom legislation in the House.
  • Sen. John Fetterman (D-PA) broke with his party to support the ballroom project.
  • Democrats remain broadly opposed to the ballroom construction, citing Trump's demolition of parts of the White House without congressional authorization and concerns about corruption from private funding.
  • A federal judge had previously blocked construction of the ballroom earlier in April on grounds that Trump had not secured authorization from Congress.
  • Trump had previously said the ballroom would be funded by private donations.
